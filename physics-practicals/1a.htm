Introduction to Diodes

Diodes are semiconductor devices that allow current to flow in one direction while blocking it in the other direction. This behavior makes diodes fundamental components in electronic circuits, serving functions like rectification, signal demodulation, and voltage regulation.

Types of Diodes

In this study, we focus on two common types of diodes:

  • Silicon (Si) Diodes: Represented by IN 4007 in our experiment
  • Germanium (Ge) Diodes: Represented by OA 79 in our experiment

Key Characteristics

The main characteristics that differentiate these diodes include:

  • Knee/Threshold Voltage: The voltage at which the diode starts conducting significantly (typically 0.7V for Si and 0.3V for Ge)
  • Forward Resistance: The resistance when conducting in forward bias
  • Reverse Saturation Current: The small current that flows when the diode is reverse biased
  • Ideality Factor: Indicates how closely the diode follows the ideal diode equation

A basic diode circuit with forward bias configuration

Diode Theory and Principles

The behavior of diodes is governed by the Shockley equation, which relates the current through a diode to the voltage across it:

I = Is(eqV/nkT - 1) = Is(eV/nVT - 1)

Where:

  • I = Diode current
  • Is = Reverse saturation current
  • q = Charge of an electron (1.6 × 10-19 C)
  • V = Voltage across the diode
  • n = Ideality factor
  • k = Boltzmann's constant (1.38 × 10-23 J/K)
  • T = Temperature in Kelvin
  • VT = Thermal voltage (kT/q ≈ 26 mV at room temperature)

Ideality Factor (n)

The ideality factor is a measure of how closely a diode follows the ideal diode equation:

n = (q/kT) × Slope = (1/VT) × Slope

where Slope is the slope of the ln(I) vs V graph.

Forward Bias Characteristics

When a diode is forward biased, the positive terminal of the power supply is connected to the anode and the negative terminal to the cathode. In this configuration, current can flow through the diode once the applied voltage exceeds the threshold voltage.

Determining Threshold Voltage

The threshold (knee) voltage is the voltage at which the diode starts conducting significantly. On a V-I curve, it's where the curve begins to rise steeply.

Forward Resistance

The forward resistance of a diode can be calculated using:

rf = ΔV/ΔI

This is the slope of the V-I curve in the conducting region, and it's typically a very small value.

Reverse Bias Characteristics

When a diode is reverse biased, the negative terminal of the power supply is connected to the anode and the positive terminal to the cathode. In this configuration, only a very small current (the reverse saturation current) flows through the diode until the breakdown voltage is reached.

Reverse bias configuration for measuring diode characteristics

Experimental Procedure

  1. Reverse the polarity of the diode
  2. Connect to the power supply
  3. Increase the voltage in steps of 0.5V
  4. Record the (very small) diode current
  5. Choose the appropriate current range on your measuring instrument
  6. Switch off the supply after taking sufficient readings

Reverse Saturation Current

The reverse saturation current (Is) is the small current that flows when the diode is reverse biased. It's an important parameter in the diode equation and is temperature dependent:

  • For silicon diodes: typically in the nA (nano-ampere) range
  • For germanium diodes: typically in the µA (micro-ampere) range

Experimental Setup and Procedure

To accurately measure and analyze the V-I characteristics of diodes, a proper experimental setup is essential.

Required Apparatus

  • Battery or DC power supply (0-30V)
  • Rheostat (variable resistor)
  • Voltmeter
  • Milliammeter (for forward bias measurements)
  • Microammeter (for reverse bias measurements)
  • Resistance box
  • Keys/switches
  • Silicon diode (IN 4007)
  • Germanium diode (OA 79)
  • Connecting wires
